ESEM

ESEM

= a new type of SEM, which allows the examination of specimens in the presence of gases.

=> wet and dry, insulating or conducting and, generally, all specimens can now be viewed with no or minimal preparation, in their natural state, or in vacuum.

ESEM…some history

The SEM’s primary limitations:

  • restrictions on samples by requiring high vacuum sample environment.
  • samples have to be clean, dry and electrically conductive.
  • Nonconductive specimen have to be coated with conductive film to avoid specimen charging.

The ESEM was developed in the mid eighties. Its primary advantages lie in permitting the microscopist to vary the sample environment through a range of pressures, temperatures and gas compositions.

ESEM…some history

ESEM: all of the performance advantages of a conventional SEM, but without high vacuum constraint on the sample environment

=> Wet, oily, dirty, non-conductive samples may be examined in their natural state without modification or preparation.

ESEM offers high resolution SE imaging in a gaseous environment of practically any composition, at pressures as high as 50 Torr, and temperatures as high as 1500 °C.

ESEM…some history

The origin of ESEM is directly linked to the high vacuum needed in electron microscopes, that introduce restrictions on the way that certain specimens are prepared and imaged.

Very early in the history of electron microscopy, studies were related to the possibilities of imaging specimens in a more ‘‘natural’’ state.

Research by Danilatos and Robinson in the 1970s led to the first SEM capable of maintaining a relatively high pressure, removing the need to dry and coat the specimens.

The term ‘‘environmental’’ SEM was introduced in 1980.

ESEM…some history

It was generally perceived that a gaseous environment would deteriorate the resolution by an assumed broadening of the electron beam and exclude the SE detection mode of imaging

The introduction of gas in an EM is completely opposed to the generally established ideas of a clean high vacuum.

ESEM…some history

2 main problems had to be resolved:

  • reduced resolution => electron probe diameter remained small at elevated pressure
  • exclusion of SE detection => invention of gaseous detection device (GDD) => , this GDD was called GSED (for gaseous secondary electron detector) to insist on the fact that it is possible to detect secondary electrons in ESEM

ESEM…some history

ESEM became a stable instrument.

By the late 1980s, the first commercial ESEMs were being produced, opening up a world of possibilities for observing untreated specimens.

ESEM has rapidly gained acceptance by the scientific, technical and industrial community (↑ number of publications)

Summary: ESEM differs from conventional SEM mainly by:

  • Presence of a gas in the specimen chamber.
  • Samples are thus not viewed under high vacuum but under a deteriorated or ‘‘low’’ vacuum.

Example: In situ ESEM microscale salt crystallization experiments

Both Na- and Mg-sulfate can occur as hydrated phases

=> their study using conventional SEM is not feasible

ESEM enables the observation of different phases of hydrated salts at high magnification and without changing their hydration state, morphology and/or habit + allows high magnification, real-time observations of changes underwent upon crystallization and/or hydration/dehydration events

habit and size distribution of sulfate crystals, as well as microtextural and morphological modifications of the salts when they dissolve and recrystallize, can be studied using an ESEM.

ESEM example

Step 1:

  • Sample of Savonnière stone of 5x5x3 mm
  • Sample fixed on Peltier stage (cooling stage) in ESEM
  • Look at sample under dry conditions (20°C, 1.2 mbar = 5% RH)
  • Save positions of interest

ESEM example

Step 2:

  • Open ESEM
  • Surround sample with wet cloth (to avoid evaporation)
  • Add salt solution of 1,4 molality with pipette at bottom sides of sample
  • Cover sample with wet cloth (not touching the sample) to create RH ~100%RH
  • Wait 5 min, check if upper surface is wet (normally capillary uptake only takes 1/2 min for this sample size)
  • Cool down to 5°C under wet cloth (~100%RH), wait 15 min.
  • Remove wet cloths and Put sample back in ESEM, pump to 6.4 mbar at 5°C (= 73.5%RH): look at difference at saved positions
  • At end: dry at 2 mbar (8.5% RH)
